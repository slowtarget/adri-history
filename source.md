The Middle Ages in the Iberian Peninsula: Al-Andalus and the Christian Kingdoms

1. The Visigothic Kingdom of Toledo

The Visigoths arrived in Europe inthe 5th century, at the same time as the other Germanic
peoples. They first settled in southeastern Gaul, until the Battle of Vouillé (507), when King
Alaric Il was defeated by King Clovis |. From then on, the Visigoths settled in the Iberian

Peninsula, with their capital in Toledo.

Civil wars were common among them, and external intervention, such as that of the Byzantine
Empire, sometimes determined the outcome of the conflicts. In fact, Byzantine influence was
readily apparent at court, as the kings sought to consolidate their power by following the

example of the emperors. This is especially true of kings like Leovigild and Reccared.

Their religion was Christianity, but not Catholicism. They initially believed in Arianism, a heresy
that rejects the Holy Trinity. However, with King Reccared and the Third Council of Toledo

(589), the kingdom converted to Catholicism.

Unlike other kingdoms, the Visigothic kingship was elective, not hereditary; that is, it did not

pass from father to son. This, however, was the cause of many of the civil wars they endured.

During the reign of Witiza (702-710), a crisis arose that affected the economy and agriculture,
and asa result, another king was elected: Rodrigo. In the civil war, Witiza sought the help of a
foreign power, and therefore, General Tariq defeated Roderic at the Battle of Guadalete (711).

From then on, the Visigothic Kingdom would disappear, and Al-Andalus would be established.
2. Al-Andalus

## 2.1 Introduction

When Rodrigo was proclaimed king, the discontented nobles requested military aid from the
Muslims. Musa, the governor of the Umayyad Caliphate in North Africa, sent a Berber army to
the Iberian Peninsula under the command of Tariq, who confronted Roderic at the Battle of

Guadalete (711), defeating the Visigothic king, who lost his life in the battle.

Shortly afterward, Musa arrived on the Peninsula with a large army of Berbers and Arabs who,
in three years, conquered almost the entire Peninsula. They only encountered resistance in the

northwest of the Peninsula, where the Battle of Covadonga (722) took place between the

Astures, led by the Visigoth Pelayo, and a group of Muslim soldiers. After the defeat of the
Visigoths, the Muslims abandoned the area and attempted to move south into France but were

defeated by the Franks at the Battle of Poitiers (732).
The Muslim conquest was swift and effective due to several factors:

- The disintegration of the Visigothic army after the Battle of Guadalete
- The effectiveness of the Muslim soldiers, who were motivated by their faith
- The Muslims’ tolerant policies toward the Christian and Jewish populations

- The alliance between the Muslims and some Visigothic nobles
## 2.2 Political Evolution of Al-Andalus
Dependent Emirate (711-756)

After the defeat at Poitiers, the Muslims focused on controlling the Iberian Peninsula. They
occupied the entire territory except for the Cantabrian Mountains and the Pyrenees. Al-
Andalus became a province of the Umayyad Caliphate of Damascus, governed by an emir and
with its capital in Cordoba.

The invaders belonged to two different groups: the Berbers and the Arabs. Relations between
the two were strained; the Arabs despised the Berbers, and this led to numerous civil wars
between them. Furthermore, encouraged by the successes in the north of the Peninsula, some
natives started revolts in Muslim-held afeas. The Emirate was on the verge of collapse, but the

arrival of Abd al-Rahman | consolidated Islamic power in the Iberian Peninsula.

Independent Emirate (756-929)

In 750, a rebellion by the Abbasids resulted in the deaths of all members of the Umayyad family
except one: Abd al-Rahman. He fled to al-Andalus, where he had support, and in 756
proclaimed himself independent emir. This meant that, politically, he separated from the

Abbasid emir of Baghdad but continued to recognize his religious authority.

allying himself with Muladi, that is, native converts from the Iberian Peninsula. He also

Caliphate of Córdoba (929-1031)

In 929, Emir Abd al-Rahman II! proclaimed himself caliph, the highest political and religious
authority of Al-Andalus, thus definitively breaking away from the Abbasid power of Baghdad.
The new caliph was the son of a Cordoban nobleman and a Christian slave of Basque origin.
Although he had to face many revolts, he was able to halt the Reconquista and established
diplomatic relations with the Byzantine Empire and the Holy Roman Empire. He also patronized
the arts and sciences, expanded the Great Mosque of Córdoba, and built the palace of Medina

Azahara.

This was the golden age of Al-Andalus, due to increased trade with Africa and Europe, which
generated immense wealth. Cordoba, the capital, became the most important city in Western

Europe.

Abd al-Rahman II was succeeded by the caliphs Al-Hakam II and Hisham II. When the latter
was a child, the power of the caliphate fell into the hands of Almanzor, the most important
general of the time. He reformed the army of Al-Andalus and carried out 52 raids on Christian
lands and cities such as Salamanca, Barcelona, Coimbra, and Zaragoza. In fact, the nickname
“al-Mansur” means “the victorious.” He is known for his extreme cruelty and Islamic fanaticism,
which led him to destroy the cathedral of Santiago de Compostela and force Christian slaves

to carry the church bells as trophies to Córdoba.

Almanzor is also one of those responsible for the fall of the Caliphate, since by turning the
caliph into a puppet, he undermined the ruler's prestige, and by trying to ensure his son's

succession, he provoked civil wars.
Taifa Kingdoms (1031-1085)

With the death of Almanzor, power struggles began among governorsand militaryleaders, and
in 1031 the last caliph was deposed. al-Andalus fragmented into small, independent kingdoms
called taifas. These weak and small kingdoms were often at war with each other and formed

alliances with Christian kingdoms, which imposed tribute payments called parias in exchange

for not being attacked. The Christian kingdoms used this influx of money to become more
powerful than their Muslim enemies and began to advance southward. In 1085, Alfonso VI of
León and Castile conquered Toledo, forcing the Taifa kings to seek help from the Almoravid

Empire.

North African Empires (1085-1238)

The Almoravids, a religious movement that sought to restore the purity of Islam by radicalizing
the precepts of the Quran, had established an empire in northwest Africa. In 1086, they
defeated Alfonso Viat the Battle of Sagrajas, but the Andalusians were unhappy with their new
rulers, who brought with them an open and cruel persecution of Christians. The Mozarabs*
appealed to Alfonso | the Battler, King of Aragon, for help. He united thousands of Christians
into a single army and they succeeded in defeating the Almoravids at the Battle of Lucena

(1125).

This led to a new North African invasion in 1145: the Almohads, who seized control of most of
al-Andalus. They were not well received by either the Andalusians or the Christians, whom they
defeated at the Battle of Alarcos (1195). However, their power in al-Andalus did not last long,
as they were defeated at the Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa (1212). This battle marked the final

decline of al-Andalus.
Nasrid Kingdom of Granada (1238-1492)

A few years after Las Navas de Tolosa, only one Muslim territory remained on the Iberian
Peninsula: the Nasrid Kingdom of Granada. The payment of tribute to the Christians and its
natural border in the Baetic System allowed it to survive until 1492, However, after Granada
was besieged by the Christian advance, on January 2, 1492, the last king, Boabdil, surrendered
and handed over the keys to the city.

3. The Christian Kingdoms of the Iberian Peninsula

After the Muslim victory at the Battle of Guadalete (711), the Visigothic kingdom disappeared,
and part of Its population took refuge in the mountainous regions of northern Iberia. It was

there that pockets of resistance arose, which would later give rise to the Christian kingdoms.

Itis important to note that relations between these kingdoms varied over the years; there were
periods of peace and others of war, each acting according to its own interests, with alliances
that constantly shifted. They fought among themselves and against Al-Andalus, and sometimes

some of them joined forces with the Muslims to wage war against them.

The monarchs who ruled these kingdoms were considered the owners of the territories, so
when a monarch died and left several children, it was not uncommon for the kingdom to be

divided among them so they could inherit it.
Definition of Reconquista

The term “Reconquista” refers to the process of Christian reconquest of the lands occupied by
the Muslims after the destruction of the Visigothic Kingdom of Toledo. This process lasts from
711 to 1492, although it was practically completed in the 13th century with the Battle of Las

Navas de Tolosa,
Kingdom of Asturias - Kingdom of Leén

In the Cantabrian region, Christians felt threatened by the constant raids of the Muslims, and
it was then that Pelayo emerged, a military leader who took command and fought the Muslims
at the Battle of Covadonga (722). The Christian victory gave rise to the Kingdom of Asturias,
with Pelayo, the victor, as its first monarch. The capital was in Cangas de Onis, although it was

soon moved to Oviedo.

The Asturian kings took advantage of periods when the Muslim territory suffered internal
problems to expand their territories. Three monarchs stood out: Alfonso | (739-757), who
extended his domains into Galicia and began to occupy the northern part of the Duero Basin
Alfonso Il (791-842) strengthened the king's power against the nobles. During his reign, the
remains of the Apostle James were discovered in Compostela. Following this discovery, the
king ordered the construction of a church to venerate the apostle's tomb, and from the 11th
century onwards, pilgrimage routes were established leading from various points in Europe to

Santiago de Compostela.

Alfonso tlt (866-910) conquered Coimbra and Porto, and upon his death, divided his

possessions into three kingdoms for his three sons: Asturias, Galicia, and Leon. His second son,

Ordofio II (910-924), who inherited Galicia, reunited the kingdoms and moved the capital to

León. From this point on, it would be known as the Kingdom of León.

In the 10th century, with the proclamation of the Caliphate, conquering lands in the south
became more difficult for the Kingdom of León, especially due to the strength and violence of
war generals like Almanzor. However, there were still victories over Muslim forces, such as the

Battle of Simancas in 939, where King Ramiro Il defeated the army of Abd al-Rahman Ill.

Most attacks came from the eastern territories, and therefore many castles were built to keep
the kingdom safe. Because of the large number of castles, this land would come to be known

as Castile.

The owners of these castles were counts, and they were responsible for protecting the
Kingdom of Leén from Muslim invasions. Since they were far from the king's authority, they
gradually gained more independence, until Count Fernan Gonzalez (931-970) unified all the

counties and created the County of Castile, with himself as its ruler.

Guadalquivir Valley and conquer Córdoba and Seville. His son, Alfonso X, the Wise, reduced
military activity.

By the end of the 13th century, Muslim territory was limited to the Nasrid Kingdom of Granada.

Christian Kingdoms in the East of the Iberian Peninsula

Christians also found refuge in the Pyrenees. In this territory, they had to fight against the
Franks in the north and the Muslims in the south, Charlemagne had to fight the Muslims to
defend his southern border, and while doing so, he also conquered many territories in the
Pyrenees. In this area, to strengthen the border, he created a defensive zone called the Marca
Hispanica.

After Charlemagne's death, the counts of the Marca Hispanica began to distance themselves
from Frankish authority and become increasingly Independent. This led to the creation of the

Kingdom of Pamplona, the Counties of Aragon, and the Catalan Counties.
